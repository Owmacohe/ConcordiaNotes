Code Quest



Brief description: Code Quest is a top-down adventure RPG with programming-related puzzles and themes. The player advances through levels, beating bosses and improving user stats. Levels get progressively more difficult as the story progresses. There are four levels.

Design process: In the first days of production my partner and I worked on our project plan for the game, hashing out art themes, game mechanics, and general details. Secondarily, we worked primarily on game tutorials and cutscenes, allowing us to get familiar with the game we were making. Once the tutorials were completed, we spent time working on more complex elements such as inventory systems, saving player info, and working on the initial design of combat. The most challenging element in the design process was combat programming. At first we were unable to achieve our desired method, but after some consultation with our instructor, we were able to develop a way of getting what we wanted. The last half of our production primarily involved making new levels based on the existing templates we had already created, removing bugs, and user testing. In all, the production took about a month and a half. We’re proud with the work we accomplished.







Waiting Room



Brief description: Waiting Room is a simple 3D level exploration environment made in an effort to teach myself the basics of the Blender Game Engine. Each level is thematically unique, one leading to the next. The player must traverse and explore all 5 unique levels to reach the end.

Design process: Before designing this environment, I spent time following various tutorials and guides for the game engine. It was a new tool for me, and I therefore wanted to fully understand its capabilities. Later, I spent more time planning and organizing the game itself, which included sketches, a demo game, and a title screen. With assets and ideas in mind, I set to work on modeling objects and scene elements. After these initial stages I had implemented my models into the game, added my physics, and performed testing. The project took three weeks in total. More time would have would have given me more opportunity to develop the game further. I do feel Waiting Room to be a good first effort.





Bone chandelier



Brief description: A chandelier sculpture inspired by the Sedlec ossuary architecture. The chandelier is made of 3D-printed deer and human bones, which I modeled, and includes a simple wire support column. As part of an assignment to assign meaning to abstract objects, the bones became important not only because of their structural support, but also the status I game them by their unusual implementation here. A reliquary traditionally houses an important bodily part of a religious Digure, giving holy nature to it. I attempted to convey ‘created significance’ with my piece and its components.

Design process: I started work on the assignment by creating rough drafts and possible ideas to Dit the reliquary-themed requirements. I had originally thought to construct a modern reliquary of my own, but logistical difDiculties lead to this Dinal idea instead. After a week of modeling and creating test bones, I began the process of printing Dinal parts (the bones). A large setback at Dirst was 3D print quality of my bones, but after some printer settings changes and remodeling of bones, I was successful. In all, I modeled 6 different bone designs, and printed 41 components. I constructed a wire frame to act as the central core of the sculpture, and worked from there. After washing and sanding the bones, I began to affix them to the core, and to themselves. This process took a couple days, allowing the glue time to set. The project was a new experience for me and while it yielded a few setbacks, there was nothing that I wasn’t able to overcome. I feel proud of what I made, and I’m glad to have documented my process as I completed the piece.
